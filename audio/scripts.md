# Audio scripts — 5 × ~85 sec

Voice notes:
- Slow ~30% from your normal pace
- `[pause]` = 4-5 seconds of silence
- Paragraph breaks = 2-3 seconds
- If you flub, pause 4 sec and restart the sentence; I'll edit

## 1. SAFETY → `wbh-safety.m4a`

Take a breath in.

And out.

We're going to spend a few moments with the first condition: safety.

Safety isn't the absence of conflict. It's the felt sense, in your teen's body, that they are not alone in what they're facing.

[pause]

So let me ask you something. Think about your teen right now. Not the version you wish they were. The real one. The one in the next room, or in their phone, or out tonight.

When they're in something hard, even something you don't fully understand, do they have the felt sense that you are with them?

Not fixing. Not solving. Just there.

[pause]

Safety is built in small moments. The way you walk into a room. The quality of your attention. Whether they can feel your nervous system, settled.

Today, you don't have to say anything new. Just walk into the room. Let your presence say: I'm here. You are not alone in this.

That's the practice.

[pause]

See you tomorrow.

## 2. ATTUNEMENT → `wbh-attunement.m4a`

Take a breath in.

And out.

Today's condition is attunement.

Attunement is what happens when someone feels seen: not for what they're doing, but for what they're feeling underneath what they're doing.

[pause]

Your teen is rarely upset for the reason they're naming. The fight about the phone isn't about the phone. The slammed door isn't about you.

[pause]

So today, when something happens, try to listen one layer down.

If they're angry, what might be underneath the anger? Fear? Shame? Loneliness?

You don't have to name it out loud. You don't have to be right. You just have to be looking for it.

When a teenager feels that you're looking for what's actually going on with them, something in their nervous system softens. Even if their words stay sharp.

[pause]

One layer down. That's all today is.

[pause]

See you tomorrow.

## 3. SOOTHING → `wbh-soothing.m4a`

Take a breath in.

And out.

Today: soothing.

Of all the five conditions, this is the one I think parents of teens get most wrong. Not because they don't try; because they try the wrong way.

[pause]

Soothing isn't telling your teen they're okay. It isn't reassurance. It isn't logic.

Soothing is letting your own nervous system be the thing that calms the room.

Before words. Before advice. Before solutions.

[pause]

If your teen is dysregulated and you go in dysregulated, you've just added to the storm. If you go in settled, you become a place they can land.

So today, before you enter a difficult conversation, take 90 seconds for yourself first. Three slow breaths. Feet on the floor. Notice your own jaw.

You are the intervention. Your regulation, before any words you say, is the medicine.

[pause]

Regulation before relationship. That's the whole practice.

[pause]

See you tomorrow.

## 4. EXPRESSED DELIGHT → `wbh-delight.m4a`

Take a breath in.

And out.

Today's condition is expressed delight.

This is the one parents skip. We feel it; we don't say it. We assume our teen knows.

They don't.

[pause]

Teenagers are scanning, all day, for evidence of whether they are wanted. Not loved as an obligation; wanted as a person. There's a difference, and they feel it.

[pause]

So today, somewhere in the next twenty-four hours, find one specific thing about your teen that genuinely moves you. Not something they did well. Something about who they are.

The way they laugh. Their loyalty to a friend. The strange thing they're obsessed with. How they hold their coffee.

And then, in some small way, let them know you noticed.

It doesn't have to be a speech. A sentence is enough. Sometimes a look is enough.

[pause]

Delight, expressed out loud, wires something deep. Do it today.

[pause]

See you tomorrow.

## 5. EXPLORATION → `wbh-exploration.m4a`

Take a breath in.

And out.

Today: exploration.

This is the condition we forget when we're scared. And we're often scared.

[pause]

Exploration is your teen's right and need to move outward. Into new ideas. Into friendships. Into their own becoming. Even when that becoming looks nothing like what we imagined.

[pause]

The instinct, when we're scared, is to pull them in closer. To narrow the field. To make them safer by making them smaller.

But security and exploration aren't opposites. Securely attached teens explore more, not less. Because they trust there's a place to come back to.

You are the place they come back to.

[pause]

So today, instead of asking what they did wrong, or what you need to know, try asking what they're interested in. What they're noticing. What they're thinking about that has nothing to do with you.

And then listen, without redirecting.

[pause]

A wide world, and a steady home. That's the whole condition.

[pause]

See you tomorrow.
